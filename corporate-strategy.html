Layer 3 Strategic Asset

Corporate Strategy

The architecture of a multi-business organization. Answering the fundamental question: "Which businesses should we be in, and how do they relate to each other?"

What is Corporate Strategy?

Distinct from Business Strategy (how to compete within one business), Corporate Strategy is the set of decisions and actions at the holding company level.

Portfolio

Which businesses to compete in.

Relatedness

How they relate to each other.

Allocation

Distributing capital and talent.

Governance

Managing multiple trade-offs.

Framework 1

The Ansoff Matrix

Evaluating four fundamental growth strategies based on identifying existing and new products and markets.

Market Penetration

Low Risk

Existing Products + Existing Markets

Grow within existing business (more customers, higher usage, taking share from competitors).

Best when you have a strong market position and predictable business.

Product Development

Med Risk

New Products + Existing Markets

Sell new or adjacent products to your existing customer base.

Best when you have deep customer relationships and leverageable infrastructure.

Market Development

Med Risk

Existing Products + New Markets

Adapt and sell your current products to new geographies or new customer segments.

Best when you have strong product-market fit and a scalable model.

Diversification

High Risk

New Products + New Markets

Build/acquire new businesses. Highest risk, requires new operational competencies.

Best when core is declining and clear synergies (brand, channels) exist.

Framework 2

BCG Portfolio Matrix

Allocating resources across your business portfolio based on market growth and market share.

STARS

High Growth · High Share

Rapidly expanding market where you are winning. Future profit engine.

Action: Invest aggressively to maintain share.

QUESTION MARKS

High Growth · Low Share

Fast market, but you aren't winning yet. High uncertainty.

Action: Invest heavily to turn into Star OR exit early.

CASH COWS

Low Growth · High Share

Mature market where you dominate. Core profit generator today.

Action: Harvest profits, minimize investment, fund Stars.

DOGS

Low Growth · Low Share

Declining market where you aren't winning. Drags on resources.

Action: Divest, shut down, or minimal harvest.

Value Creation: Why Own Multiple Businesses?

1. Operational Synergies

Sharing infrastructure (IT, HR), manufacturing, distribution, or R&D to achieve cost reduction through scale.

Beware: Sometimes "synergy" just results in a slow, bureaucratic headquarters instead of agility.

2. Portfolio Optimization

Allocating capital to the highest-return businesses, diversifying risk, and optimizing debt and tax structures.

Beware: Retail investors can diversify stock portfolios themselves; you must be a superior capital allocator (e.g. Warren Buffett).

3. Strategic Linkages

Transferring best practices and sharing capabilities/customer data to create network effects across all products.

Beware: Forced linkages can severely damage distinct businesses that need divergent strategies.

Strategic Choices & Trade-offs

1. Focus vs. Diversification

Focus Strategy

Compete in a narrow space. Excellent for creating deep expertise and fast decision making, but vulnerable to disruption.

Example: McDonald's (Food only)

Diversification Strategy

Compete across multiple businesses. Excellent for risk reduction and new growth engines, but raises management complexity.

Example: Samsung (Everything)

2. Organic Growth vs. M&A

Organic Growth (Build)

Develop internally. Ensues culture alignment and sustainable tech capabilities, but takes a huge amount of time.

M&A (Acquire)

Buy existing execution. Extremely fast to market and instant customer base, but astronomically expensive and carries massive integration risks.

3. Vertical Integration

Owning and controlling more aspects of your own supply chain.

  • Backward Integration: Owning your own suppliers (e.g. Apple designing its own chips). Ensures quality, but removes flexibility.
  • Forward Integration: Owning your own distribution/dealerships (e.g. Tesla). Captures margins directly, but requires massive capital overlay.

Major Case Studies

Apple

Product Dev → Diversification

Moved decisively away from computer cash cows to mobile. Did not pursue cost synergy, but shared user experience DNA and platform lock-in across products.

Amazon

Market Dev → Ecosystem

Diversified into Cloud (AWS) to monetize huge internal infrastructure. Built unshakeable ecosystem lock-in (Prime + AWS + Retail) and ruthlessly eliminated failing initiatives.

Google (Alphabet)

Conglomerate via M&A

Acquired YouTube & Android early to secure distribution for Search/Ads. Organized under Alphabet to attempt unrelated diversification (Healthcare, Auto), with mixed success.

Kodak

Portfolio Failure

Invented digital photography but refused to fund it to protect their film Cash Cow. Failure to cannibalize their own product allowed competitors to destroy them entirely.

Toyota

Related Diversification

Launched Lexus and Hino Trucks. Stayed strongly inside their competence core (transportation manufacturing) yielding enormous operational synergy without risking brand dilution.

Berkshire Hathaway

Conglomerate Done Right

A deliberate, entirely unrelated portfolio strategy based solely on having Warren Buffett's super-allocator skill. Acquired operations are granted 100% autonomy.

The Cardinal Rules

DO'S

  • Clarify the Strategy

    Explicitly decide exactly what businesses you will compete in and document the boundaries.

  • Leverage REAL Synergy

    Ensure synergies are mathematically measurable before acting. Avoid theoretical spreadsheet synergies.

  • Build Ecosystem Lock-in

    Make businesses interlock naturally so customers incur massive switching costs trying to leave.

  • Allocate Capital Differently

    Stars need investment; Cows need harvesting. Never treat them with identical growth KPIs.

DON'TS

  • Pursue False Synergies

    Forced integrations ruin the uniqueness of acquired assets and drive talented staff away immediately.

  • Blind Unrelated Diversification

    Entering foreign domains without a superior capital allocator leads to severe "conglomerate discounts".

  • Protect the Cash Cow to Death

    Cannibalize your own products before competitors do. Respect that all products eventually become Dogs.

  • Assume Competence Transfers

    Success in software doesn't mean success in hardware. Always assume adjacent segments are 80% divergent.
