CRISIS MANAGEMENT: Quick Reference Guide

Crisis Management is the process of preparing for, detecting, and responding to existential threats to organizational survival or reputation. Unlike incidents (service outages, data breaches), crises threaten the organization's fundamental viability—existential threats like bankruptcy, scandal, regulatory shutdown, product catastrophe, or loss of trust. Crisis management requires speed, clarity, and often difficult trade-offs between stakeholder interests.

Why It Matters:

  • Survival is at stake: Poor crisis response can destroy a company; good response can preserve it
  • Trust is fragile: Stakeholders lose trust in seconds; rebuilding takes months
  • First 24 hours decide: Initial response sets narrative; hard to correct if wrong early
  • Complexity increases under pressure: Crisis requires clear decision-making when emotions run high
  • Prevention beats response: Most crises are preventable with foresight and preparation

1. Situational Crisis Communication Theory (SCCT)

Source: W. Timothy Coombs (2007). Strategy must match Crisis Type and Reputation Threat Level.

  • Victim Crisis (Low Responsibility): Natural disaster, product tampering.
  • Accidental Crisis (Moderate Responsibility): Technical failure, accident.
  • Intentional Crisis (High Responsibility): Misconduct, ignoring safety standards.

2. Mitroff's Onion Model of Crisis Readiness

Source: Pauchant & Mitroff (1992). Crisis readiness layers: 1. Psychology (Core), 2. Culture, 3. Structure, 4. Strategy.

3. High Reliability Organization (HRO) Principles

Source: Weick & Sutcliffe (2001). Managing the unexpected via preoccupations with failure, reluctance to simplify, sensitivity to operations, and deference to expertise.

Phase 1: Preparation & Prevention (Pre-Crisis)

  • Build resilient systems; identify signals through Pre-mortems; designate leadership with delegated authority.

Phase 2: Detection & Diagnosis (Early Crisis)

  • Address the "Signal Problem"; distinguish between incident and crisis; define scope and moral dimension.

Phase 3: Containment & Stabilization (Active Crisis)

  • Match response to responsibility level (SCCT); Acknowledge → Investigating → Timeline; take decisive action.

Phase 4: Recovery & Healing (Post-Crisis)

  • Return to normal; provide support to victims; close the "Trust Gap" with consistent action.

Phase 5: Learning & Resilience (Long-Term)

  • Double-loop learning (fix culture, not just process); update the organizational Psychology layer.

1. J&J Tylenol (1982) - The Gold Standard

What Happened: Seven deaths in Chicago due to cyanide-laced bottles. A Victim Crisis.

Strategic Moves: Stakeholder primacy (safety > profit); recalled 31M bottles ($100M cost); pioneered tamper-resistant packaging.

Result: Market share recovered within a year; became most trusted company in healthcare.

Key Lesson: Over-responding on the moral dimension in a Victim Crisis builds massive reputational capital.

2. Boeing 737 MAX (2018-2019) - Cultural Failure

What Happened: Two crashes, 346 deaths. Caused by MCAS software hidden from pilots to save on training costs.

Strategic Failures: Simplified explanation ("pilot error"); hid technical flaws from regulators (Intentional Crisis); leadership invisibility and leaglistic tone.

Result: $30B+ loss; CEO fired; brand trust severely damaged for years.

Key Lesson: An Intentional Crisis requires a "Rebuild" strategy. Boeing used "Diminish," leading to collapse.

3. BP Deepwater Horizon (2010) - Leadership Failure

What Happened: Massive oil spill; 11 deaths. Environmental catastrophe.

Strategic Failures: Minimization of spill size; externalizing blame to contractors; "I'd like my life back" gaffe by CEO.

Result: $65B+ in cleanup/legal fees; brand synonymous with negligence.

Key Lesson: Leadership visibility must be paired with Empathy. Without empathy, visibility is toxic.

✅ DO 1: Move from Structure to Culture

What: Build a culture where "bad news travels fast" and whistleblowers are rewarded.

Why: Crises aren't secrets; employees at the bottom usually know months before failure.

Good Example: Airline rewarding pilots for reporting "near misses" without punishment.

Bad Example: Managers hiding production delays to meet quarterly bonuses.

✅ DO 2: Apply SCCT Matching Principle

What: Pick a communication strategy that matches your responsibility level (Victim, Accidental, Intentional).

Why: Misaligned strategy (e.g. Diminish for Intentional) is seen as lying.

Good Example: Admitting technical failure (Accidental) and offering rebuild/apology immediately.

Bad Example: Blaming "rogue engineers" for a systemic scandal (Volkswagen).

✅ DO 3: Prioritize HRO Principles

What: Treat every minor incident as a potential signal of a systemic crisis.

Why: Crises are accumulations of small, ignored signals.

Good Example: Hospital treating a "wrong-patient-med" error as a reason to redesign entire workflow.

Bad Example: Ignoring 50 complaints because "customers always complain."

❌ DON'T 1: Allow Psychological Denial

What: Don't let leadership believe "It can't happen to us."

Why: Hubris leads to delayed detection and slow response.

Good Example: CEO setting a "Red Team" to find vulnerabilities annually.

Bad Example: "We are the global leader; our planes don't crash." (Boeing internal sentiment).

❌ DON'T 2: Treat Crisis as a PR Problem

What: Don't focus on "managing the message" until you have "managed the problem."

Why: Transparency without action is PR; action without transparency is invisible.

Good Example: Recalling product first, then holding detailed press briefings.

Bad Example: Hiring a PR firm to release statements while the unsafe product is still on shelves.
