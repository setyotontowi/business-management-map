Vision & Mission: Comprehensive Framework Guide with Practical Tools

Overview

Vision & Mission are foundational strategic artifacts that define organizational purpose, direction, and identity. This guide covers 6 major frameworks + North Star Metric, with practical templates for SIMRS.

1. COLLINS & PORRAS: BHAG & HEDGEHOG CONCEPT

Framework Overview

Jim Collins & Jerry Porras (Built to Last, 2002) distinguish between:

Vision
  • Core Purpose (timeless reason for existence, never fully achieved)
  • Core Values (what we stand for, don't change)
  • Big Hairy Audacious Goal (BHAG) (10-30 year target, audacious but achievable)
Hedgehog Concept (Focus)

The intersection of:

  1. What you can be best at (core competencies)
  2. What drives your economics (what makes money)
  3. What you're passionate about (what energizes the team)

BHAG Characteristics

  • Stands for 10-30 years
  • Clear and compelling
  • Focuses efforts and investments
  • Universally understood
  • Audacious (seems unrealistic but achievable with effort)

Example BHAGs from History

  • Nike (1960s): "Crush Adidas"
  • Intel (1970s): "Put a computer on every desk and in every home"
  • Amazon (2000s): "Earth's most customer-centric company"

For SIMRS: Potential BHAG

Current (too vague):

"Become the leading healthcare digital ecosystem in Southeast Asia"

Better BHAG (Collins & Porras style):

"By 2034, be the backbone of healthcare delivery for 10,000+ healthcare facilities across Southeast Asia, serving 100M+ patients annually through integrated data platform"

Why this works:

  • ✅ Audacious (10,000 facilities vs. current ~100s)
  • ✅ Measurable (specific numbers)
  • ✅ Long-term (10 years)
  • ✅ Compelling (enables better patient care at scale)
  • ✅ Clear (everyone understands the target)

Hedgehog Concept for SIMRS

What you can be best at:

  • Healthcare process digitization in emerging markets
  • Local expertise (Indonesia regulatory, workflows, relationships)
  • Systems integration (connecting disparate healthcare organizations)

What drives your economics:

  • Licensing/subscription from hospitals, clinics, pharmacies
  • Premium for integration services and customization
  • Data analytics as revenue stream (anonymized, aggregated healthcare insights)

What you're passionate about:

  • Improving healthcare access and quality in Indonesia
  • Reducing administrative burden on healthcare workers
  • Making healthcare data usable for better decisions

→ Hedgehog Focus for SIMRS:

Build integrated healthcare platform for emerging markets where local expertise + integration capabilities + passion for healthcare access converge

Practical Application

Document for SIMRS:


CORE PURPOSE (timeless):
Make healthcare more efficient and accessible through technology

CORE VALUES (don't change):
1. Patient-centricity
2. Local expertise & relationships
3. Ethical data stewardship
4. Operational excellence

BHAG (10-30 year target):
[Use the SIMRS example above]

HEDGEHOG CONCEPT:
[Best at: Local healthcare digitization]
[Drives economics: Healthcare licensing + integration premiums]
[Passionate about: Healthcare access in emerging markets]

2. DRUCKER'S MANAGEMENT BY OBJECTIVES (MBO)

Framework Overview

Peter Drucker (The Practice of Management, 1954) argues mission should answer:

  1. What is our business? (What do customers see us doing?)
  2. Who are our customers? (Not who we want, but who actually buys)
  3. What do customers value? (What problem do we solve?)
  4. What will our business be? (Where are we heading?)
  5. What should our business be? (Where do we want to go?)

Mission Template (Drucker-style)


What is our business?
- SIMRS: Healthcare information system for hospital operations management

Who are our customers?
- Primary: Hospital administrators, IT directors, clinical staff
- Secondary: Clinics, pharmacies, insurers (emerging)

What do customers value?
- Operational efficiency (faster billing, inventory management)
- Data accessibility (better clinical decision-making)
- Regulatory compliance (hospital accreditation requirements)
- Local support (not dependent on US vendor)

What will our business be? (3-5 year horizon)
- Integrated healthcare platform serving hospitals + clinics + pharmacies
- Connected data ecosystem (patient flows between facilities)
- Emerging: Data analytics, telemedicine, insurance claims

What should our business be? (10+ year aspirational)
- Universal healthcare data platform for emerging markets
- Trusted intermediary between patients, providers, payers
- Standard infrastructure for healthcare digitalization in SE Asia

Why Drucker's Approach Works

  • ✅ Forces clarity on who you actually serve (not who you wish to serve)
  • ✅ Links business definition to customer value, not internal capability
  • ✅ Distinguishes "will be" (realistic projection) vs. "should be" (aspirational)
  • ✅ Encourages periodic reassessment ("What will our business be?")

3. SENGE'S SYSTEMS THINKING & SHARED VISION

Framework Overview

Peter Senge (The Fifth Discipline, 1990) emphasizes:

Shared Vision is not a single statement, but:

  • Individual visions + aspirations
  • Collective agreement on desired future
  • Continual conversation and refinement
  • Living document that evolves with learning

Senge's Distinction

  • Vision Statement (words on paper) ≠ Shared Vision (lived experience)
  • Shared vision emerges through dialogue, not top-down decree

For SIMRS: Shared Vision Process

Rather than executives writing mission in isolation:

  1. Individual Conversations (1:1s)
  • Ask each leader/team: "What impact do you want to have? What does success look like?"
  • SIMRS developer: "I want to build systems that actually reduce nurses' workload"
  • Hospital customer: "I want data that helps me make better decisions about patient care"
  • Product manager: "I want to build products that matter in healthcare"
  1. Synthesis (find common threads)
  • Themes emerge: reducing burden on healthcare workers, enabling better decisions, improving patient care
  • Articulate collective vision from these themes
  1. Iterative Refinement (shared dialogue)
  • Present back to organization
  • Invite feedback, challenge, refinement
  • Live document (revisit quarterly)

Senge Vision Statement Structure

Not: "We will be the largest healthcare IT vendor in SE Asia by 2034"

Instead:

"We are building a world where healthcare workers have the data and tools they need to make decisions with confidence, where administrative burden is minimized, and where patient care is improved through intelligent systems. This journey is shared with our customers, partners, and team."

Why Senge's Approach Matters

  • ✅ Drives deeper engagement (everyone contributed)
  • ✅ More adaptable (shared vision evolves with learning)
  • ✅ Links personal purpose to organizational purpose
  • ✅ Builds psychological safety (your voice was heard)

4. KAPLAN & NORTON: STRATEGY MAP & BALANCED SCORECARD

Framework Overview

Robert Kaplan & David Norton (The Balanced Scorecard, 1992) connect vision to execution through four perspectives:

Four Perspectives:
  1. Financial (How do we sustain profitability?)
  2. Customer (What value do customers perceive?)
  3. Internal Processes (What processes must we excel at?)
  4. Learning & Growth (How do we build capabilities?)

Strategy Map Structure


VISION: [Articulated BHAG or mission]
        ↓
FINANCIAL PERSPECTIVE
- Revenue growth from new products (clinic, pharmacy)
- Margin expansion (reduce support cost per customer)
- Profitability targets (% gross margin)
        ↓ (enabled by)
CUSTOMER PERSPECTIVE
- Hospital retention (low churn)
- Clinic/pharmacy adoption (new segments)
- Customer satisfaction (NPS > 50)
        ↓ (enabled by)
INTERNAL PROCESSES
- Faster product delivery (time to MVP)
- Seamless integrations (clinic ↔ hospital ↔ pharmacy)
- Scalable operations (support more hospitals without linear cost increase)
        ↓ (enabled by)
LEARNING & GROWTH
- Talent development (hiring for new product domains)
- Innovation culture (psychological safety, experimentation)
- Knowledge management (learning from failures, successes)

For SIMRS: Balanced Scorecard Example


VISION: "By 2034, backbone of healthcare delivery for 10,000+ SE Asian facilities"

FINANCIAL OBJECTIVES & METRICS
├─ Objective: Maximize hospital profitability
│  └─ Metrics: Revenue per hospital, gross margin %, churn rate
├─ Objective: Build new healthcare product revenue streams
│  └─ Metrics: Clinic product MRR, pharmacy product MRR, time to revenue
└─ Objective: Achieve economies of scale
   └─ Metrics: Support cost per customer, % revenue from self-serve

CUSTOMER OBJECTIVES & METRICS
├─ Objective: Deliver superior value to hospitals
│  └─ Metrics: NPS, feature adoption rate, training completion %
├─ Objective: Win clinic market
│  └─ Metrics: Clinic NPS, implementation time, ROI realization
└─ Objective: Expand to pharmacy/insurer segments
   └─ Metrics: Partner acquisition cost, ecosystem adoption

INTERNAL PROCESS OBJECTIVES & METRICS
├─ Objective: Deliver products fast with high quality
│  └─ Metrics: Sprint velocity, bug escape rate, time to MVP
├─ Objective: Integrate seamlessly across healthcare providers
│  └─ Metrics: Integration coverage (% of healthcare transactions), API uptime
└─ Objective: Scale operations without proportional cost increase
   └─ Metrics: Support tickets per person, customer onboarding efficiency

LEARNING & GROWTH OBJECTIVES & METRICS
├─ Objective: Build dynamic capabilities (sensing, seizing, transforming)
│  └─ Metrics: # of new market experiments, time to validate, learning velocity
├─ Objective: Foster innovation culture
│  └─ Metrics: % time on experimentation, psychological safety score, ideas generated
└─ Objective: Develop talent for new healthcare segments
   └─ Metrics: Retention of key talent, cross-functional collaboration, external hiring success

Why Balanced Scorecard Works

  • ✅ Connects vision → strategy → execution
  • ✅ Balances short-term (financial, customer) with long-term (learning, growth)
  • ✅ Forces clarity on cause-and-effect (processes enable customer value, which drives financial)
  • ✅ Provides metrics to track progress

5. SIMON SINEK: GOLDEN CIRCLE (WHY/HOW/WHAT)

Framework Overview

Simon Sinek (Start With Why, 2009) argues most organizations communicate backwards:

Most organizations say:


WHAT: We build hospital management software
HOW: Using integrated architecture, local expertise
WHY: [Unclear or weak - "to make money" isn't inspiring]

Inspiring organizations say:


WHY: We believe healthcare workers should have the tools and data they need to make better decisions
HOW: By building integrated systems that reduce administrative burden
WHAT: Therefore, we build SIMRS and healthcare platforms

Why/How/What for SIMRS

WHY (Belief/Purpose):

"We believe that healthcare is a fundamental right, and that technology can make healthcare better by reducing burden on healthcare workers and enabling better decisions for patients. We believe emerging markets deserve the same quality digital infrastructure as wealthy countries."

HOW (Values/Approach):

"We achieve this by:

  • Deeply understanding local healthcare workflows (not importing Western solutions)
  • Building systems that are simple, reliable, and locally supported
  • Connecting healthcare facilities so data flows where it's needed
  • Being relentless about usability (healthcare workers don't have time for complicated software)"

WHAT (Products/Services):

"Therefore, we build:

  • SIMRS (Hospital Management System)
  • Clinic management software
  • Pharmacy networks
  • Healthcare data platform
  • All integrated, all local, all reliable"

Why Sinek's Approach Resonates

  • ✅ Inspires employees ("I'm not coding, I'm improving healthcare")
  • ✅ Attracts customers with shared values (hospitals that care about data integrity)
  • ✅ Differentiates in crowded market (not competing on features, competing on mission)
  • ✅ Survives commodity competition (if product becomes commoditized, mission keeps customers)

6. NORTH STAR METRIC

Framework Overview

North Star Metric (popularized by Sean Ellis, Reforge) is the single most important metric that represents long-term value creation and sustainable growth.

Characteristics of a Good North Star

  • ✅ Leads to revenue and profitability long-term
  • ✅ Reflects core customer value
  • ✅ Measurable and trackable
  • ✅ Single metric (not composite)
  • ✅ Influenced by product and team actions
  • ✅ Accessible (not trailing indicator like annual revenue)

Common North Star Metrics by Business Model

Business ModelNorth Star
SaaS (enterprise)Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR) or # of active paying customers
SaaS (marketplace)Gross Merchandise Volume (GMV) or # of transactions
Social networkDaily/Monthly Active Users
E-commerceRevenue or customer lifetime value
HealthcarePatient outcomes or provider satisfaction

For SIMRS: North Star Metric Options

Option 1: Healthcare Facility Coverage

  • Metric: Number of healthcare facilities actively using SIMRS ecosystem
  • Why: Reflects market penetration, ecosystem adoption, long-term durability
  • Drawback: Doesn't distinguish hospital vs. clinic vs. pharmacy
  • Target: 10,000 facilities by 2034 (connects to BHAG)

Option 2: Healthcare Transactions Processed

  • Metric: Number of monthly healthcare transactions (patient visits, prescriptions, claims, referrals) flowing through SIMRS platform
  • Why: Reflects ecosystem density, data value, network effects
  • Drawback: Large number, hard to track
  • Target: 100M transactions/month by 2034

Option 3: Healthcare Workers Enabled

  • Metric: Number of active healthcare workers using SIMRS daily
  • Why: Direct measure of impact (improving work for healthcare workers)
  • Drawback: Trailing indicator (hard to predict)
  • Target: 500K healthcare workers daily active

Option 4: Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR)

  • Metric: Total monthly subscription revenue from all SIMRS products
  • Why: Reflects business sustainability, customer value perception, growth
  • Drawback: Pure financial, doesn't reflect healthcare impact
  • Target: $10M+ MRR by 2034

Recommendation for SIMRS:

Use dual North Stars:

  1. Healthcare Facility Adoption (reflects mission, market penetration)
  2. Monthly Recurring Revenue (reflects sustainability, profitability)

Both should grow in tandem (more facilities = more revenue). If MRR grows but facility adoption stalls, you're extracting more from fewer customers (unsustainable). If facility adoption grows but MRR stagnates, you're not creating value customers will pay for.

North Star Metric Cascade

Once you define North Star, cascade it down:


NORTH STAR: 10,000 healthcare facilities + $10M MRR by 2034

YEARLY MILESTONES:
- 2025: 500 facilities, $500K MRR
- 2026: 1,000 facilities, $2M MRR
- 2027: 2,500 facilities, $5M MRR
- 2028: 5,000 facilities, $8M MRR
- 2029: 10,000 facilities, $15M MRR

QUARTERLY TARGETS (for 2025):
- Q1: 450 facilities, $400K MRR
- Q2: 475 facilities, $450K MRR
- Q3: 500 facilities, $500K MRR
- Q4: 530 facilities (ahead of plan), $550K MRR

MONTHLY TARGETS:
- Facility growth: +20-30 new facilities/month
- MRR growth: +$30-50K/month
- Churn: <2% monthly (critical—losing customers hurts both metrics)

LEADING INDICATORS (predict North Star):
- # of product trials started
- # of clinics/pharmacies in discovery phase
- # of new healthcare segments explored
- Customer onboarding time (faster onboarding = faster revenue realization)
- Feature adoption (better adoption = better retention = sustained revenue)

Alignment Example: If North Star is "10K Facilities + $10M MRR"

What does this mean for Product decisions?

  • ✅ Build features that other facility types (clinic, pharmacy) want → facilities growth
  • ✅ Improve implementation process → faster customer onboarding → faster MRR
  • ✅ Expand integrations → more valuable to customers → lower churn
  • ❌ Build features only 1 hospital wants → doesn't move North Star
  • ❌ Spend engineering on cost reduction if it sacrifices growth

What does this mean for Sales?

  • ✅ Target clinics and pharmacies (new facility types) → facilities growth
  • ✅ Increase pricing for mature hospitals (if value-justified) → MRR growth
  • ✅ Reduce churn through customer success → sustained MRR
  • ❌ Focus on features discounting if it hurts perceived value
