# CRISIS MANAGEMENT: Quick Reference Guide

## What It Is

Crisis Management is the process of preparing for, detecting, and responding to existential threats to organizational survival or reputation. Unlike incidents (service outages, data breaches), crises threaten the organization's fundamental viability—existential threats like bankruptcy, scandal, regulatory shutdown, product catastrophe, or loss of trust. Crisis management requires speed, clarity, and often difficult trade-offs between stakeholder interests.

**Why It Matters:**
- **Survival is at stake**: Poor crisis response can destroy a company; good response can preserve it
- **Trust is fragile**: Stakeholders (customers, employees, investors, public) lose trust in seconds; rebuilding takes months
- **First 24 hours decide**: Initial response sets narrative; hard to correct if wrong early
- **Complexity increases under pressure**: Crisis requires clear decision-making when emotions run high
- **Prevention beats response**: Most crises are preventable with foresight and preparation

## Academic Frameworks

### 1. Situational Crisis Communication Theory (SCCT)
**Source**: W. Timothy Coombs (2007)
**Core Concept**: Crisis response strategy must match the **Crisis Type** and the **Reputation Threat Level**.
- **Crisis Types**:
    - **Victim Crisis**: Organization is also a victim (Natural disaster, workplace violence, product tampering). *Low responsibility.*
    - **Accidental Crisis**: Unintentional actions (Technical failure, accident). *Moderate responsibility.*
    - **Intentional Crisis**: Purposeful violation of law/standards (Human error, management misconduct). *High responsibility.*
- **Response Strategies**: Deny (not recommended if true), Diminish (excuses/justification), Rebuild (apology/compensation), Bolster (remind of past good deeds).

### 2. Mitroff's Onion Model of Crisis Readiness
**Source**: Pauchant & Mitroff (1992)
**Core Concept**: Crisis management is built through four layers.
1. **Psychology (Core)**: Individual belief systems and defense mechanisms (Denial "It can't happen here").
2. **Culture**: Group beliefs (Openness vs. Secrecy).
3. **Structure**: Formal crisis teams, protocols, and hierarchies.
4. **Strategy**: Specific crisis playbooks and technical capabilities.
*Insight: Most companies focus on Structure/Strategy but fail due to flawed Psychology/Culture.*

### 3. High Reliability Organization (HRO) Principles
**Source**: Weick & Sutcliffe (2001)
**Core Concept**: Managing the unexpected by building resilience into daily operations.
- **Preoccupation with failure**: Treating any small error as a signal of a systemic vulnerability.
- **Reluctance to simplify**: Avoiding "human error" explanations; digging into complex system interactions.
- **Sensitivity to operations**: Leaders being highly aware of front-line technical realities.
- **Deference to expertise**: Decisions are made by those with the most expertise, not the highest rank, during crisis.

## The 5 Phases of Crisis Management

### Phase 1: Preparation & Prevention (Pre-Crisis)
- **HRO Shift**: Move from "planning for a list of risks" to "building a resilient system."
- Identify potential crises through environmental scanning and "Pre-mortems."
- Develop crisis leadership teams with **delegated authority** to act without board approval in the first 12 hours.

### Phase 2: Detection & Diagnosis (Early Crisis)
- **The Signal Problem**: Most crises have faint early signals (e.g., Boeing's MCAS concerns).
- Rapidly distinguish between a "Technical Incident" and an "Organizational Crisis."
- **Goal**: Move from "this is bad" to "here is the scope and moral dimension."

### Phase 3: Containment & Stabilization (Active Crisis)
- **SCCT Match**: Immediately identify your responsibility level.
- Activate communication protocols: Acknowledge → Investigating → Timeline for update.
- **Decisive Action**: J&J Recalled ALL bottles; BP's leadership initially minimized the leak (Failure).

### Phase 4: Recovery & Healing (Post-Crisis)
- Return to normal operations while providing support (legal, financial, emotional) to victims.
- **The Trust Gap**: Rebuilding trust requires visible, consistent action over time (Commitment).

### Phase 5: Learning & Resilience (Long-Term)
- Thorough "Double-Loop Learning": Don't just fix the process; fix the culture/incentives that broke it.
- Update the organization's "Psychological Layer" in Mitroff's Onion.

## Crisis Case Study 1: J&J Tylenol (1982) - The Gold Standard

**What Happened:**
Seven people died in Chicago after consuming Tylenol laced with cyanide. It was a **Victim Crisis** (tampering by unknown third party), but J&J's reputation was at stake.

**Strategic Moves:**
- **Stakeholder Primacy**: CEO James Burke prioritized customer safety over financial loss immediately.
- **Massive Recall**: Recalled 31 million bottles ($100M cost) nationwide, even though tampering was local.
- **Radical Transparency**: Co-operated fully with media; held daily press briefings.
- **Innovation-Led Recovery**: Invented and pioneered triple-seal "Tamper-Resistant" packaging, which became the industry standard.

**Result:**
Market share (which dropped from 37% to 7%) returned within one year. J&J became the most trusted company in healthcare for decades.

**Key Lesson:**
In a Victim Crisis, **over-responding** on the moral dimension builds massive reputational capital.

## Crisis Case Study 2: Boeing 737 MAX (2018-2019) - The Cultural Failure

**What Happened:**
Two 737 MAX aircraft crashed within 5 months, killing 346 people. The cause was a software system (MCAS) designed to mimic old flight characteristics to save on pilot training costs.

**Strategic Moves (Failures):**
- **Reluctance to Simplify**: Boeing initially blamed "foreign pilots" rather than the system design (Violated HRO Reluctance to Simplify).
- **Inadequate Disclosure**: Boeing hid the existence of MCAS from pilots and regulators to speed up certification (Intentional Crisis per SCCT).
- **Leadership Invisibility**: CEO Dennis Muilenburg was criticized for legalistic, cold statements that prioritized stock price over victim safety.
- **Delayed Transparency**: It took months and a global grounding by China (not the US) to force an acknowledgment of the systemic flaw.

**Result:**
- Market value lost $30B+; CEO forced to resign; $2.5B settlement with DOJ.
- Federal oversight increased; brand trust severely damaged (still struggling in 2024).

**Key Lesson:**
An **Intentional Crisis** (prioritizing profit/speed over safety) requires a "Rebuild" strategy (full apology/remorse/compensation). Boeing used a "Diminish" strategy, which led to reputational collapse.

## Crisis Case Study 3: BP Deepwater Horizon (2010) - Leadership Failure

**What Happened:**
An oil rig explosion killed 11 workers and caused the largest accidental marine oil spill in history.

**Strategic Moves (Failures):**
- **Minimization**: CEO Tony Hayward initially minimized the spill size ("Modest") and the impact ("The Gulf is a very big ocean").
- **Externalizing Blame**: BP blamed the rig owner (Transocean) and the cementer (Halliburton).
- **The "I'd like my life back" Gaffe**: Tony Hayward expressed personal frustration in a way that made him look insensitive to the victims and the environmental catastrophe.

**Result:**
BP paid $65B+ in cleanup and legal fees. The brand remains synonymous with environmental negligence for a generation.

**Key Lesson:**
Leadership visibility must be paired with **Empathy**. Without empathy, visibility is toxic.

## Do's & Don'ts (Comprehensive Guide)

### DO 1: Move from Structure to Culture (Mitroff Layer 2)
**What:** Build a culture where "bad news travels fast." Reward whistleblowers and transparency.
**Why:** Most crises aren't secrets; people at the bottom know about them months before leaders do (e.g., Boeing engineers knew MCAS was risky).
**Example:**
- ✅ **Good**: An airline rewards pilots for reporting "near misses" without punishment. High data connectivity ensures safety.
- ❌ **Bad**: Managers hide production delays to meet quarterly bonuses, leading to a massive safety recall later.

---

### DO 2: Apply Coombs' SCCT Matching Principle
**What:** Identify your responsibility level (Victim, Accidental, Intentional) and pick the right communication strategy.
**Why:** Using a "Diminish" strategy for an "Intentional" crisis (like Boeing) is perceived as lying and destroys trust.
**Example:**
- ✅ **Good**: An accidental data breach occurs; company admits technical failure immediately, offers compensation, and explains the fix (Rebuild).
- ❌ **Bad**: A company caught faking emissions (Volkswagen) claims it was "a few rogue engineers" (Diminish).

---

### DO 3: Prioritize HRO Principles (Preoccupation with Failure)
**What:** Treat every "minor incident" as a potential signal of a systemic crisis.
**Why:** Crises rarely happen overnight; they are the accumulation of small, ignored signals.
**Example:**
- ✅ **Good**: A hospital treats a single wrong-patient-medication error (with no harm) as a systemic failure, redesigning the entire workflow.
- ❌ **Bad**: Leadership ignores 50 complaints about a product part because "customers always complain." (Part later causes a fatal accident).

---

### DON'T 1: Allow Psychology Layer Denial (Mitroff Layer 1)
**What:** Don't let leadership succumb to "It can't happen to us" or "Our reputation is too strong."
**Why:** Overconfidence (Hubris) leads to delayed detection and slow response.
**Example:**
- ✅ **Good**: CEO sets a "Red Team" to find the 3 things that could destroy the company this year.
- ❌ **Bad**: "We are the global leader in aviation; our planes don't crash." (Boeing internal sentiment prior to 737 MAX).

---

### DON'T 2: Treat Crisis as a PR Problem (Damage Control)
**What:** Don't focus on "managing the message" until you have "managed the problem."
**Why:** Transparency without action is PR; action without transparency is invisible. Both fail.
**Example:**
- ✅ **Good**: Recalling product first, then explaining the reason.
- ❌ **Bad**: Hiring a high-end PR firm to release "leadership statements" while the unsafe product is still on shelves.

---

### DON'T 3: Centralize Information, Decentralize Blame
**What:** Don't hoard information at the top while blaming the bottom (scapegoating).
**Why:** Crisis management requires **Expertise-Led Decisions** (HRO Principle), not hierarchy-led silos.
**Example:**
- ✅ **Good**: CEO listens to the head of Engineering on whether to ground the fleet, regardless of stock price.
- ❌ **Bad**: CEO tells PR to blame "Human Error" by the operator while ignoring the technical design flaw reported by staff.

## Crisis Management Maturity Model

| Level | Mindset | Strategy | Resilience |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Reactive** | Crisis is a surprise/bad luck | Scapegoating + Denial | Fragile (high risk of collapse) |
| **Compliant** | Crisis is a checklist | Legal/PR-led statements | Brittle (recovering slowly) |
| **Proactive** | Crisis is a risk to manage | Structural playbooks + Training | Robust (contained quickly) |
| **Resilient (HRO)** | Crisis is a learning source | Cultural transparency + System redesign | Antifragile (grows stronger) |

---

## Crisis Response Decision Matrix (SCCT)

| Attribution | Strategy | Actions |
|---|---|---|
| **Low (Victim)** | Deny / Bolster | Legal defense, Remind of history |
| **Moderate (Accidental)** | Diminish / Justify | Explain technical context, minimize blame |
| **High (Intentional)** | **Rebuild / Apologize** | Admit guilt, Full compensation, Leadership change |

## Quick Summary

- **Core Goal**: Build a culture (Mitroff Layer 2) that detects signals early and responds with transparency matched to responsibility (SCCT).
- **Critical Mindset**: Stakeholder safety > Short-term profit; Systems fix > Scapegoat.
- **The HRO Secret**: Don't just plan for the "big one"; obsess over the "small ones" that signal the "big one."
- **Biggest Mistake**: Hubris (Denial) leading to delayed transparency and defensive communication.
